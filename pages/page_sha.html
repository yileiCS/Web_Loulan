Loulan in the Desert

Loulan Ancient City is located in Ruoqiang County, Bayingolin Mongol Autonomous Prefecture, Xinjiang Uygur Autonomous Region of China1. It sits at the eastern edge of the Taklamakan Desert, the world's second-largest shifting sand desert2. The climate here is extremely arid, making it one of the regions with the least precipitation in the world, with annual rainfall typically less than 20mm, while annual evaporation exceeds 3000mm3.

The relentless erosion by desert sand was one of the main reasons for Loulan's decline4. With climate change and alterations in the hydrological environment, the Peacock River changed its course, causing the Loulan oasis to gradually dry up and eventually be swallowed by the shifting sands. Today, this once-prosperous Silk Road hub is deeply buried beneath the yellow sand, with only a few architectural ruins exposed above the surface, silently telling the story of its past glory5.

Wind Erosion and Site Preservation

Continuous wind erosion poses a serious threat to the Loulan ruins6. Strong winds carrying sand particles constantly scour the ancient city's remaining walls, accelerating the weathering and damage to the site. Archaeologists have found that some architectural structures and artifacts discovered in the early 20th century have now completely disappeared or been severely damaged7.

Facing the challenges of the natural environment, the Chinese government and research institutions have implemented a series of protective measures: establishing dedicated archaeological stations for regular monitoring and recording of the site; using modern technological means such as 3D scanning and digital modeling for virtual preservation8; and setting up windbreaks in key areas to mitigate wind erosion. Despite these efforts, the protection of Loulan ruins still faces enormous challenges and requires the joint attention and cooperation of the international community9.

Archaeological Discoveries in the Desert Environment

While the extremely arid desert environment accelerates the weathering of the site, it has also, to some extent, aided in the preservation of artifacts, especially organic materials10. In Loulan and surrounding areas, archaeologists have discovered well-preserved wooden architectural components, textiles, paper documents, and even mummies like the "Loulan Beauty"11, providing valuable physical evidence for studying the history and culture of the ancient Silk Road.

As more of Loulan's ruins in the desert are excavated, people gain a deeper understanding of this mysterious ancient city and its historical environment12. However, with the impact of climate change and human activities, the preservation condition of the site is increasingly worrisome. Protecting this precious cultural heritage has become an important task for contemporary archaeology and cultural relic preservation13.
